Timing: When to Reach Out

Timing collapses third-party intent and first-party engagement into one layer, weighted by recency (45-day half-life). For a sales team making coverage decisions this week, a 6sense Decision-stage surge and a VP visiting the pricing page twice are functionally the same signal.

Hiring deserves its own call-out. A new VP or Head of CX in the last 90 days, or 3+ CX agent postings in 30 days, is a reliable leading indicator that a stack review is coming. Most models bury hiring inside engagement. For Gladly it's too strong to bury.

Confidence: How Much to Trust the Data

Every field that feeds Fit or Timing carries a confidence flag, because no single vendor wins across every attribute. A Waterfall Enrichment strategy (Clay orchestrates this) runs vendors in sequence — only spending credits on the next vendor when the prior one returns nothing or a low-confidence match.

Field Primary Source Fallback Conflict Rule
Firmographics ZoomInfo Clearbit / Breeze Most recent verified_at wins. Delta >20% → RevOps review queue
Technographics BuiltWith HG Insights Additive (Shopify + Zendesk coexist). HG supplies incumbent contract dates
Buying Stage 6sense Bombora direct 6sense authoritative for stage; Bombora is upstream feedstock
Contact (email/mobile) ZoomInfo (US) / Cognism (EU) Apollo Emails re-verified by SMTP before CRM write

Three Governance Rules

01

Delta Threshold

If a new vendor value has a >20% delta from the current CRM value, it routes to a RevOps manual review queue instead of silently overwriting.

02

Rep Lock

Rep-verified values are locked. A weekly refresh cannot overwrite a hand-corrected tag. Human signal beats machine signal.

03

Audit Log

Every write logs {field, old, new, source, timestamp, confidence} — defensible to Sales, Security, and Legal in the same room.

The constraint that shapes the whole design: The native Marketo–Salesforce sync is one-way for Accounts (SFDC → Marketo). Marketo reads account fields but only writes to Leads and Contacts. Therefore the master score must live in Salesforce. Scoring is authored in SFDC (reverse ETL from a warehouse, or a scoring engine writing directly). Marketo's job becomes activation: reading Tier (A/B/C/D), firing smart campaigns, and routing alerts.

~$400–$1,250/month at 500 accounts/month vs. $10K+ for a 6sense-led stack. 6sense becomes a V3 conversation once we have closed-won data to train against.

Why Claude API drives sales adoption

For each scored account, Claude generates Score_Rationale__c — a plain-text paragraph the AE sees directly on the Account record. Example:

"Tier A because 2,500 employees, Shopify Plus, currently on Zendesk, three CX roles posted in 30 days, and a new VP of CX started in June."

That paragraph is the single biggest difference between a scoring model reps fight and one they trust. AI should augment human sellers, not replace them.

06

How We Know It's Working

Weekly (Leading)
MQA → Opp conversion by tier — target Tier A: 30–50%, Tier C: <10%
Speed to lead — time from MQA to first AE touch
Meeting set rate per MQA — by tier and source signal
Quarterly (Lagging)
Win rate by tier — does Tier A close materially better?
ACV by tier — are A-tier accounts larger deals?
Cycle length by tier — faster close for higher-fit accounts?
Pipeline velocity sliced by signal — which signals lift revenue vs. generate noise?

The discipline most teams skip: A 5–10% random holdout routed ignoring the score. If Tier A accounts don't convert meaningfully better than the holdout, the model is wrong — and that's a RevOps problem, not a Sales problem.

A note on how I used AI for this brief

I worked the problem before opening any AI tool. Gladly's positioning, the assessment, and the job description shaped the framework first. With the argument in shape, I ran Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity in parallel for three jobs: customer detail on Gladly's install base, pressure-testing weightings, and current vendor pricing. Every output got cross-checked, and judgment calls stay with me.
